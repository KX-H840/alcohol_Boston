Massachusetts' Complex Relationship with Liquor Regulation

2024.12

In the shadow of Boston’s bustling and diverse neighborhoods, licenses and regulation reveal a complex tapestry of economic ambition and public health challenges. Massachusetts stands at a crossroads, where the promise of economic growth through alcohol sales collides with the stark realities of community well-being.

The state’s approach to alcohol regulation is a high-stakes balancing act that pits economic opportunity against potential social harm. As Massachusetts continues to expand liquor licenses based on public policy bases-particularly in underserved communities- experts warn that each new outlet carries profound consequences beyond simple business metrics, potentially exacerbating issues of domestic violence, addiction and community health.

This sentiment encapsulates the complex relationship Massachusetts has with alcohol regulation- a system that has long puzzled residents and visitors alike. As the state continues to grapple with balancing economic growth, public health, and social equity, the debate over liquor licensing has intensified, revealing deep-seated issues that go far beyond the simple act of selling alcohol.

At the heart of this debate lies a fundamental question: How can Massachusetts foster economic growth through alcohol sales while simultaneously protecting public health and addressing inequities in different neighborhoods? The answer, as experts and community leaders are finding, is far from simple.

How to get a Liquor License in Greater Boston?

According to Andrew Napolitano, Deputy Communications Director, Office of State, Treasurer and Receiver General of the ABCC, obtaining a liquor license in Greater Boston involves a detailed process governed by both local and state regulations. The process begins with determining the type of liquor license needed, such as for a restaurant or retail store. Applicants must check the availability of licenses in their desired area, as Boston operates under special legislation known as home rule petitions. Next, presenting the proposal to local neighborhood groups is essential, as their feedback is considered before the city reviews the application. After submitting the application to the City of Boston licensing board, applicants must attend a public hearing where community input is gathered. If approved locally, the application moves to the Alcoholic Beverage Control Commission (ABCC) for final vetting. Throughout this process, demonstrating public need and common good is crucial, emphasizing the importance of community engagement and compliance with regulations.

The Unique Landscape of Massachusetts Liquor Regulation

Massachusetts’ approach to alcohol regulation is rooted in a complex history that has resulted in a system many find confusing and, at times, contradictory. The state’s Alcoholic Beverage Control Commision (ABCC) oversees all aspects of alcohol manufacturing, distribution, and sales, implementing strict licensing requirements and regulations that shape the industry’s landscape.

Andrew Napolitano, explained how authorizing liquor licenses works and is regulated: “We oversee every license in the state... There are two types: retail licenses and state licenses... The local municipality, so the city of Boston, approves the licenses, and then what ABCC does is we license the business and vet the individuals.”

Dr. David H. Jernigan, from Boston University School of Public Health, offers a stark reminder of alcohol’s unique position in society: “Alcohol isn’t breakfast cereal. It is a very particular product. It is our favorite drug. It is our cheapest drug, and it’s already our most available drug, although I’m worried about the increasing presence of cannabis.”

This perspective underscores the challenges facing regulators and policymakers. While alcohol sales undoubtedly contribute to local economies, the health and social implications are significant and often overlooked in the licensing process.

“These downstream consequences of alcohol use are seldom considered in licensing decisions," Dr. Jernigan points out. "These decisions are primarily made in my experience on economic grounds, on the grounds of promoting local business, and the burden on the community is not included in the conversation."

The Economic Argument vs. Public Health Concerns

The economic benefits of alcohol sales are clear. They provide jobs, generate tax revenue, and can contribute to neighborhood revitalization. However, these benefits come with substantial public health costs that are often less visible but no less impactful.

Napolitano also clarified that licenses are not guaranteed. “A license is a privilege. It's not a guarantee, and with that privilege come responsibilities... We look for public safety, serving someone over the age of 21, and serving an intoxicated individual.”

The consequences of alcohol misuse are severe and far-reaching. Dr. Jernigan emphasizes, "the biggest ones are likely to be unseen and difficult to track, but it's domestic violence, interpersonal violence, [and] child abuse."

These social costs are often not factored into licensing decisions, creating a disconnect between economic policy and public health outcomes.

Inequity in License Distribution

One of the most contentious issues in Massachusetts’ alcohol regulation is the inequitable distribution of liquor licenses. Historically, wealthier communities have had more control over the number of licenses in their areas, leading to a concentration of outlets in less affluent neighborhoods.

Dr. Jenigan explains this phenomenon: "They cluster there for a couple of reasons. One is that the wealthier communities tend to have the political power to limit the outlets. And the second is in at least some places, their long histories of redlining of banks prioritizing loans for alcohol businesses in poor and minority neighborhoods with the encouragement of the federal government."

This uneven distribution has led to recent efforts to increase the number of liquor licences in underserved areas. In autumn, over 200 new licenses were added, targeted at low-income communities. However, Dr. Jernigan expresses concern about this approach: "It's an experiment. I'm worried because they're putting the licenses in places that don't have much else going on, and that is a recipe for trouble."

The Impact on Vulnerable Communities

The concentration of liquor stores in less affluent areas raises significant concerns about health equity. Dr. Jernigan notes, "What we find over and over again is that the same amount of alcohol will cause much more damage in a poor community than it will in a wealthier one." This disparity is due to various factors, including limited access to healthcare, fewer social support systems, and economic stress.

East Boston, a predominantly Hispanic neighborhood, serves as a prime example of this complex dynamic. The area has seen an increase in liquor stores and alcohol-serving establishments in recent years, providing economic opportunities but also raising concerns about community health.

The liquor store owner in East Boston acknowledges the complexity of the situation: "It's a delicate balance. We want to run a successful business, but we also care about our community. I think there needs to be more thoughtful regulation that considers the impact on neighborhoods."

The Intersection with Homelessness

The intersection of alcohol regulation, homelessness, and alcohol abuse in Massachusetts presents a complex and multifaceted issue. While alcohol availability and homelessness are closely intertwined, their relationship is not straightforward.

Massachusetts has a high number of alcohol licenses compared to other states. According to the American Addiction Center, in 2019, the state had approximately 1.81 on- and off-premises alcohol outlets per 1000 persons, which is significantly higher than many other states. This high availability of alcohol can have serious implications for vulnerable populations, including those experiencing homelessness.

Dr. Jernigan points out, “If you increase alcohol availability in a poor community, the consequences per drink of alcohol served are going to be higher. That's been confirmed in research over and over again, simply because the safety nets aren't there."

The relationship between homelessness and alcohol use is bidirectional. Alcohol abuse can contribute to homelessness by disrupting relationships and financial stability, while homelessness often leads individuals to use alcohol as a coping mechanism for the stresses they face. Peter Ciurczak, Senior Research Analyst at Boston Indicators, provides some startling statistics: “70% of the total homeless population that we've, that was counted in 2023, were in families, either, you know, two parent families with kids or one parent family with kids.”

Research indicates that alcohol use is prevalent among homeless populations, with approximately one-third struggling with alcohol-related issues. Many face barriers to treatment, including limited access to resources and smaller social support networks. These challenges make it difficult for homeless individuals to reduce or stop their alcohol consumption, perpetuating a cycle that is hard to break.

Underlying this issue is a significant housing affordability crisis. Massachusetts ranks as the second least affordable state for renters, with Greater Boston also being among the least affordable places in the country. Kelly Turley from the Massachusetts Homeless Coalition highlights that “Massachusetts has been ranked for this year as the second least affordable state in the country for renters and [...] Greater Boston is also the second least affordable place in the country.” This crisis exacerbates the challenges faced by homeless individuals and may influence their patterns of alcohol use.

The Recovery Community's Perspective

The recovery community offers another crucial perspective on this issue. Maryanne Frangules of MOAR (Massachusetts Organization for Addiction Recovery) expresses concern about the potential impact of increased alcohol availability on those struggling with addiction or in recovery.

"Increased alcohol availability could pose significant challenges for those in recovery or at risk of addiction," Frangules notes. "We've seen from our work that easy access to alcohol can be a trigger for relapse and can make it harder for individuals to maintain their recovery."

However, Frangules also acknowledges the complexity of the issue: "We remain committed to supporting recovery and public health while recognizing the importance of economic development in our communities. As these changes roll out, we urge community members, business owners, and policymakers to work together in creating environments that foster both economic growth and wellness."

The Way Forward

As Massachusetts continues to grapple with these complex issues, it’s clear that a nuanced, comprehensive approach is needed. Dr. Jernigan suggests looking beyond alcohol for economic development: "There are so many ways to build community wealth that have nothing to do with alcohol. I think it's extremely short sighted, or cities, neighborhoods, communities, to look to alcohol to build their economies."

Andrew Napolitano from the ABCC emphasizes the importance of rigorous processes in licensing decisions: “When we approve a licence for a well-run establishment, it can become a cornerstone of a neighborhood, providing jobs and a space for community gathering. On the flip side, when we have to take disciplinary action, it's because we're committed to maintaining standards that protect public safety and community well-being."

Moving forward, Massachusetts faces the challenge of crafting alcohol policies that promote economic growth while protecting public health and addressing long standing inequities between neighborhoods. This will require a collaborative effort involving policymakers, public health experts, community leaders, and business owners.

As the debate continues, it’s crucial to keep in mind Dr. Jernigan’s warning: "Alcohol isn't breakfast cereal." It's a substance that, while economically beneficial, carries significant public health and social risks that must be carefully managed and mitigated.

The path to a more equitable and health-conscious system of alcohol regulation in Massachusetts will not be easy. It will require ongoing dialogue, data-driven decision-making, and a willingness to prioritize long-term community well-being over short-term economic gains. Only through such a comprehensive approach can Massachusetts hope to resolve its complex and often perplexing relationship with alcohol regulation.
